# AP Style Cheat Sheet

> Unofficial, paraphrased summary of widely known Associated Press (AP) style conventions.
> This is NOT a substitute for the official AP Stylebook. Used as a grounding reference for the AP Style Editor app.
> Each rule has a stable `id` the AI model must cite when applying it.

---

## Numbers

- **id: NUM-001** — Spell out whole numbers one through nine; use figures for 10 and above. Exceptions: ages, percentages, money, dates, dimensions, scores.
- **id: NUM-002** — Always use figures for ages (e.g., "a 5-year-old boy", "she is 7").
- **id: NUM-003** — Use the % sign with a numeral, no space (e.g., "5%", not "5 percent"). AP changed this rule in 2019.
- **id: NUM-004** — Spell out any number that begins a sentence, except for a calendar year ("2026 was a busy year.").
- **id: NUM-005** — Use figures and "million"/"billion" for large round numbers: "$2.4 million", not "$2,400,000".
- **id: NUM-006** — Use figures for money with a $ sign: "$5", "$2.50", "$1 million". Spell out "cents": "5 cents".
- **id: NUM-007** — Use a hyphen for compound modifiers with numbers: "a 5-year-old child", "a 10-mile hike".

## Dates and times

- **id: DATE-001** — Abbreviate months when used with a specific date: Jan., Feb., Aug., Sept., Oct., Nov., Dec. Spell out March, April, May, June, July always.
- **id: DATE-002** — Do not use "st", "nd", "rd", "th" with dates. Write "Jan. 5", not "January 5th".
- **id: DATE-003** — Spell out the month when standing alone or with only a year: "January 2026", "It happened in February."
- **id: DATE-004** — Use figures for times, with lowercase "a.m." and "p.m." with periods: "10 a.m.", "2:30 p.m." Use "noon" and "midnight" (not "12 p.m." or "12 a.m.").
- **id: DATE-005** — Avoid redundancies like "10 a.m. this morning"; use "10 a.m. today".
- **id: DATE-006** — Days of the week are not abbreviated, except in tabular form.

## Punctuation

- **id: PUNC-001** — AP omits the serial (Oxford) comma in simple series: "red, white and blue". Use it only if needed for clarity or in complex series.
- **id: PUNC-002** — Use single space after a period, not two.
- **id: PUNC-003** — Periods and commas go inside quotation marks. Dashes, semicolons, question marks, exclamation points go inside only when they apply to the quoted matter.
- **id: PUNC-004** — Use an em dash (—) with spaces around it for abrupt change or emphasis: "He stood up — and left."
- **id: PUNC-005** — Use a hyphen for compound modifiers before a noun ("well-known author") but generally not after ("the author is well known").

## Titles and names

- **id: TITLE-001** — Capitalize formal titles only when they directly precede a name: "President Jane Doe" but "Jane Doe, the president".
- **id: TITLE-002** — Lowercase job descriptions (vs. formal titles): "astronaut John Smith", "actress Mary Lee".
- **id: TITLE-003** — On second reference, use last name only. Do not use courtesy titles (Mr., Mrs., Ms., Miss) except in direct quotations or to distinguish people with the same last name.
- **id: TITLE-004** — Capitalize composition titles (books, movies, songs, plays, TV shows) and put them in quotation marks: "Gone With the Wind". Do NOT use italics. Exception: the Bible, almanacs, dictionaries, encyclopedias, handbooks, and reference works are not quoted.
- **id: TITLE-005** — Abbreviate Gov., Lt. Gov., Rep., Sen. and military titles before a full name: "Sen. John Smith". Spell out and lowercase when used alone: "the senator said".
- **id: TITLE-006** — "Dr." is used in first reference only for medical doctors (M.D., D.O., D.D.S.), not for Ph.D.s in general copy.

## Places and addresses

- **id: PLACE-001** — Spell out state names when they stand alone in body text: "She lives in California."
- **id: PLACE-002** — Abbreviate state names with AP-style abbreviations (NOT two-letter postal codes) when used with a city: "Springfield, Ill.", "Austin, Texas." Eight states are never abbreviated: Alaska, Hawaii, Idaho, Iowa, Maine, Ohio, Texas, Utah.
- **id: PLACE-003** — Some cities stand alone without a state (per AP datelines list): New York, Chicago, Los Angeles, Boston, Washington, Atlanta, Dallas, Denver, Detroit, Houston, Las Vegas, Miami, Philadelphia, Phoenix, Pittsburgh, Salt Lake City, San Francisco, Seattle, St. Louis, etc.
- **id: PLACE-004** — Use "U.S." as an adjective ("the U.S. economy") but spell out "United States" as a noun.
- **id: PLACE-005** — Abbreviate "Avenue", "Boulevard", "Street" only with a numbered address: "1600 Pennsylvania Ave." Spell out otherwise: "Pennsylvania Avenue".

## Attribution and quotations

- **id: ATTR-001** — Prefer "said" as the attribution verb. Avoid "claimed", "admitted", "stated", "noted" unless they convey real meaning.
- **id: ATTR-002** — Place attribution after the first sentence or clause of a long quote, not at the very end.
- **id: ATTR-003** — Use past tense for attribution in news writing: "she said", not "she says".
- **id: ATTR-004** — Identify the speaker before or within a quote, not buried at the end of a multi-sentence quotation.

## Common usage and word choice

- **id: USE-001** — "More than" is preferred over "over" with numerical figures: "more than 1,000 people".
- **id: USE-002** — Use "email" (one word, no hyphen). Use "internet" and "web" lowercase.
- **id: USE-003** — Use "OK" (capitalized), not "okay".
- **id: USE-004** — Use "advisor" or "adviser"? AP prefers "adviser".
- **id: USE-005** — "Affect" is generally a verb (to influence). "Effect" is generally a noun (a result).
- **id: USE-006** — "Among" for three or more; "between" for two.
- **id: USE-007** — Avoid "very", "really", and other intensifiers in news copy.
- **id: USE-008** — Use "Black" (capitalized) when referring to people in a racial, ethnic or cultural sense. Use "white" lowercase per AP (as of 2020 guidance).
- **id: USE-009** — Use gender-neutral language where possible: "firefighter" not "fireman", "spokesperson" not "spokesman" unless gender is known and relevant.
- **id: USE-010** — "Toward", not "towards". Same for "forward", "backward", "upward" (no trailing "s").

## Acronyms and abbreviations

- **id: ACR-001** — Spell out an organization or term on first reference, with the acronym in parentheses only if needed for clarity. On second reference, use the acronym alone. Avoid acronym soup.
- **id: ACR-002** — Use periods in two-letter abbreviations: "U.S.", "U.N.", "U.K." No periods in longer all-caps abbreviations: "NATO", "FBI", "CIA", "NASA".
- **id: ACR-003** — Do not introduce an acronym in parentheses if it will only appear once.

## Headlines and capitalization

- **id: HEAD-001** — AP headlines use sentence case (only the first word and proper nouns capitalized), not title case. (Note: many publications override this house-style.)
- **id: CAP-001** — Capitalize proper nouns and formal titles before names. Lowercase generic references: "the president", "the company".
- **id: CAP-002** — Lowercase "federal", "government", "administration" unless part of a formal name.

---

## How the model must cite

When proposing a change, the model MUST include an `ap_rule_id` value that matches one of the `id` fields above exactly (e.g., `NUM-003`). If no rule above applies, the model should set `ap_rule_id` to `"NONE"` and explain in `rationale` why the change is still appropriate — the app will flag these for user review.
